## Page 2: The Concept (`/about/index.html`)

**Page title:** The Concept
**Tagline:** What Consulting Club is, how it works, and why the debrief is the whole point.

---

### The idea

Most professionals spend years developing sharp analytical minds, and then take jobs that use only part of that capacity. The instinct to diagnose a situation, frame a problem cleanly, and advise a client — those muscles exist, but they can go unused for a long time.

Consulting Club is a recurring, low-stakes arena for exercising them.

Each month, one member plays Client and brings a real problem — something they're genuinely wrestling with. The rest form an ad hoc Consulting Team and deliver a structured engagement: problem framing, analysis, recommendation, all inside 90 minutes. Then everyone sits down together for a candid debrief.

No one gets hired or fired. That's the point.

---

### The format

A Consulting Club session runs roughly as follows:

**Client brief (10 min):** The Client presents the situation using a structured template — context, the core question, what they've tried, what a good outcome looks like.

**Team work (60 min):** The Consulting Team works live — framing the problem, choosing frameworks, running analysis, and building toward a recommendation. The Client is available to answer questions but stays out of the team's process.

**Recommendation presentation (10 min):** The team delivers their recommendation directly to the Client.

**Debrief (15 min):** Everyone steps back. The group reflects on what happened — what was sharp, what was soft, where the team got stuck. And each person answers the question the Client is quietly wondering anyway.

---

### The "Would I hire these people?" round

The debrief closes with a structured round: every person in the room — including the Client and observers — answers *Would I hire this team?* Not "did they have the right answer" but "would I put them in front of my actual client?"

The question is deliberately uncomfortable. It focuses the debrief on what matters in real consulting: not just whether the analysis was correct, but whether the team was organized, whether they listened, whether their communication was crisp enough to be trusted with something real.

The rule is candor over comfort — and that's what makes it developmental.

---

### Roles

Each session has five roles, rotating across members:

- **Client** — brings the problem, briefs the team, answers questions, evaluates the recommendation
- **Lead Consultant** — owns the team's process and the final recommendation
- **Analyst** — does the analytical heavy lifting; owns the data and the frameworks
- **Skeptic** — pushes back, tests assumptions, makes sure the team isn't just agreeing with each other
- **Scribe** — captures the work in real time; keeps the team on track against the clock

Everyone rotates. Playing Client is at least as valuable as playing Consultant.

---

### What it's for

Consulting Club is developmental, not transactional. Nobody is getting paid. There's no client relationship to protect. The stakes are calibrated so the room is safe enough to try things, honest enough to be useful, and real enough to matter.

For members who are curious about a pivot into consulting or advisory work — whether as a second act, a portfolio career, or just a more formal way to use their expertise — it's a no-risk test drive. You find out fast whether you enjoy the work, and you leave with a cleaner picture of what you'd need to sharpen.

## Page 4: Category Landing — Scoping & Diagnosis (`/library/scoping-diagnosis/index.html`)

**Page title:** Scoping & Diagnosis
**Tagline:** Getting to the real problem before solving the wrong one.

**Category intro (2–3 sentences):**
The most common consulting mistake isn't bad analysis — it's excellent analysis of the wrong question. This category covers the tools for slowing down at the start of an engagement: diagnosing what's really going on, structuring the problem space, and agreeing with the client on what you're actually trying to solve.

**Framework cards (in order):**

1. **Problem Framing** — "The question behind the question: how to reframe what the client is actually asking."
2. **The Double Diamond** — "Two rounds of diverge/converge: discover the real problem, then develop the right solution."
3. **Issue Trees & MECE** — "Structuring a problem so nothing overlaps and nothing is missing."
4. **Hypothesis-Driven Approach** — "Start with a provisional answer and test it — consulting's most distinctive intellectual habit."
5. **Stakeholder Mapping** — "Who has power, who has interests, and how to read a room before you walk into it."
6. **Root-Cause Tools** — "Five Whys and Ishikawa / fishbone for drilling past symptoms to the real cause."
7. **Scoping the Engagement** — "Objectives, deliverables, and the underrated discipline of saying what's out of scope."

## Page 5: Category Landing — Analysis & Frameworks (`/library/analysis-frameworks/index.html`)

**Page title:** Analysis & Frameworks
**Tagline:** The classic toolkit for making sense of a situation.

**Category intro:**
These are the frameworks that show up across industries, problem types, and career levels because they work. Some are fifty years old; all of them are still in the bag. This category covers the mechanics of each, the attribution, and — most importantly — how to actually use them in a 90-minute session.

**Framework cards:**

1. **SWOT** — "A fast, structured scan of strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats."
2. **Porter's Five Forces** — "Assessing the competitive intensity — and therefore the profit potential — of an industry."
3. **PESTEL** — "Surveying the macro environment systematically: political, economic, social, technological, environmental, legal."
4. **Value Chain Analysis** — "Mapping where value is created — and where it leaks."
5. **BCG Matrix & GE/McKinsey Nine-Box** — "Portfolio strategy across business units or product lines."
6. **Ansoff Matrix** — "Four growth moves: market penetration, market development, product development, diversification."
7. **McKinsey 7S** — "Diagnosing alignment across strategy, structure, systems, staff, skills, style, and shared values."
8. **Marketing Mix (4Ps / 7Ps) & Jobs-to-be-Done** — "Understanding what customers actually want — and whether you're delivering it."
9. **Profit / Profitability Tree** — "Decomposing a profitability problem into its drivers."
10. **Market Sizing & Estimation** — "Estimating a market from first principles when the data doesn't exist yet."

## Page 7: Category Landing — Execution, Process & Change (`/library/execution-change/index.html`)

**Page title:** Execution, Process & Change
**Tagline:** Because a recommendation no one can implement is worthless.

**Category intro:**
The graveyard of consulting engagements is full of decks that were analytically correct and practically ignored. This category covers the tools for the work after the presentation: how to assign ownership, lead change, eliminate waste, and make hard prioritization calls when resources are finite and everything feels urgent.

**Framework cards:**

1. **RACI** — "Clarifying who's responsible, accountable, consulted, and informed — before it becomes an argument."
2. **Kotter's 8 Steps & ADKAR** — "Two complementary models for leading change that actually sticks."
3. **Lean / Value-Stream Mapping & Six Sigma (DMAIC)** — "Eliminating waste and reducing variation with disciplined, systematic rigor."
4. **Prioritization** — "Impact/effort matrix, MoSCoW, and weighted scoring for making hard calls under real constraints."
