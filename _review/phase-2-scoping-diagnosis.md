## Page 1: Problem Framing
**URL:** `/library/scoping-diagnosis/problem-framing/`
**One-line definition:** The discipline of establishing the right question before attempting to answer it — understanding what the client actually needs solved, which is often different from what they initially asked.

### Overview
Problem framing doesn't have a single originator. It draws from at least three traditions: systems thinking, design practice, and consulting methodology.

Russell Ackoff, the systems theorist, was among the first to articulate the distinction rigorously. In his decades of writing on management and systems — including *Ackoff's Best* (1999) and the earlier *The Art of Problem Solving* (1978) — he argued that most management failures result from solving the wrong problem precisely rather than solving the right problem approximately. He called tangled, poorly defined problem situations "messes" to distinguish them from well-structured "problems."

In management consulting, the discipline was embedded in firm training from early on. McKinsey's approach to problem solving — documented accessibly in Ethan Rasiel's *The McKinsey Way* (1999) — puts problem definition at the front of every engagement. Barbara Minto's MECE framework (see Issue Trees) builds on the assumption that you've already framed the problem correctly; the framing step comes before the tree.

Design thinking added "how might we" reframing as an explicit technique — a way of restating a problem as an open invitation to solve it. Stanford's d.school and IDEO popularized this in the 2000s. Roger Martin's *The Design of Business* (2009) connected design thinking disciplines to strategic management for a business audience.

The core insight that runs through all of these: clients present problems in the form of solutions. "We need a new CRM" usually means "our customer data is fragmented." "We need to cut costs by 15%" may mean "our margins are eroding and we don't know why." A consultant who takes the stated problem at face value is already halfway to the wrong answer.

### When to Use It
At the very start of every engagement — before any analysis begins. Also valuable mid-engagement when the team's findings suggest the original question was poorly specified. Not a tool for later phases: if you're reframing at the synthesis stage, something went wrong at the start.

### How It Works
1. **Capture the presenting problem** exactly as the client stated it. Write it down verbatim.
2. **Ask "what would solving this achieve?"** — move from the solution the client named to the goal beneath it.
3. **Ask "what does success look like in a year?"** — surface underlying intent and expose what the client is actually optimizing for.
4. **Ask "what else could cause this symptom?"** — challenge the assumption that the stated problem is the real one.
5. **Restate the problem** as a clean "we're trying to..." sentence at the right level of abstraction: concrete enough to be solvable, broad enough to encompass the real issue.
6. **Check it back** with the client. "Yes, exactly" means you're aligned. "Well, sort of" means keep going.

Useful techniques within framing:
- **Ladder of abstraction** — move up ("why does this matter?") to find the goal, move down ("what specifically is happening?") to find the constraint. The right level is usually one step up from where the client started.
- **"How might we"** — rewrite the problem as an open invitation: "How might we help customers find the right product faster?" This shifts the room from defense to invention.
- **Five Whys applied to the problem statement** — ask why the presenting problem exists, five times, before accepting it.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
Use the first five minutes after the Client brief to extract the presenting problem as stated, then take five minutes as a team — before touching any frameworks — to challenge it. Write the original question on one side of the board and your reframed version on the other. Force a vote: are these meaningfully different? If yes, check it back with the Client before proceeding. If the Client says "that's a better question," you've already added value — and you haven't done a single analysis yet.

In a 90-minute session, this five-minute discipline saves thirty minutes of analysis headed in the wrong direction.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Reframing as performance** — changing the question for novelty rather than because it's genuinely better
- **Reframing without the client** — agreeing internally on a different question without checking it back explicitly
- **Over-abstracting** — "the real problem is your culture" is almost always unhelpful; effective reframes are still concrete and solvable
- **Skipping it entirely** — assuming the client has already done this work before coming to you. They usually haven't.

### References & Further Reading
- Ackoff, Russell L. *The Art of Problem Solving* (1978); *Ackoff's Best* (1999)
- Martin, Roger. *The Design of Business* (2009)
- Gause, Donald C. and Weinberg, Gerald M. *Are Your Lights On? How to Figure Out What the Problem Really Is* (1990) — a short, irreverent classic on exactly this problem

## Page 2: The Double Diamond
**URL:** `/library/scoping-diagnosis/double-diamond/`
**One-line definition:** A design process model that structures work as two rounds of diverge-then-converge: first to discover and define the right problem, then to develop and deliver the right solution.

### Overview
The Double Diamond was developed and published by the **Design Council** (UK) in 2005 as part of their research report *Eleven Lessons: Managing Design in Eleven Global Companies*. It emerged from studying how leading design-led organizations actually worked — and finding that they consistently moved through two distinct problem-solution cycles rather than going straight from brief to solution.

The model has since been widely adopted beyond product design into service design, business consulting, and organizational change, because it formalizes something experienced consultants already do intuitively: the discipline of not jumping to solutions before the problem is understood.

The two diamonds:
1. **First diamond — Problem space**
   - *Discover* (diverge): explore broadly, gather inputs, challenge assumptions, understand the full landscape
   - *Define* (converge): synthesize what you've learned, agree on the problem statement, write the design brief
2. **Second diamond — Solution space**
   - *Develop* (diverge): generate multiple potential solutions, prototype, experiment
   - *Deliver* (converge): test, refine, and ship the best solution

The key insight — easily stated, consistently violated — is that the first diamond is not optional. Most teams skip directly to "develop" because it feels more productive. The Double Diamond forces a visible pause between understanding the problem and solving it.

### When to Use It
When the problem is genuinely uncertain or when there's any chance the client's initial problem statement is incomplete. Especially valuable for innovation, service redesign, and strategy work where the problem space is ambiguous. Less critical when the problem is already tightly defined and solution development is the clear task — don't add process where it isn't needed.

### How It Works
**First diamond:**
- *Discover*: Talk to customers, stakeholders, or anyone affected. Observe. Gather data. Read. Stay deliberately open — you're not yet forming hypotheses, you're building a rich picture of the situation.
- *Define*: Look across your discoveries for patterns, tensions, and surprises. Write a clear problem statement (or "design brief"): what is actually going on, for whom, and what does success look like? This is the end of the first diamond.

**Second diamond:**
- *Develop*: Generate multiple solutions without premature judgment. Prototype at low fidelity. Try things in parallel. Get feedback quickly.
- *Deliver*: Select, refine, and implement the solution that best addresses the defined problem.

The model is iterative, not strictly linear. You may loop back — discovering new information during development that redefines the problem is normal and healthy, not failure.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
Use the Double Diamond explicitly as the structure for the 90-minute session. Map it on the board at the start:
- Minutes 0–10: Client brief (entering the first diamond)
- Minutes 10–30: Discover phase — ask questions, challenge assumptions, explore the problem space together
- Minutes 30–40: Define phase — agree on the reframed problem statement; write it on the board
- Minutes 40–75: Develop phase — build the analysis and recommendation options
- Minutes 75–85: Deliver phase — converge on a single recommendation

This turns the Double Diamond from an abstract model into a live meeting agenda. The Scribe should mark which phase the team is in and flag when they're jumping ahead.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Skipping the first diamond** — going directly from client brief to solution generation; produces solutions to the wrong problem
- **Treating it as linear** — the double diamond is a guide, not a script; good teams loop back when they discover something that changes the problem definition
- **Over-process in a short engagement** — in a 90-minute session, the "discover" phase may be 20 minutes of sharp questioning rather than weeks of ethnographic research; calibrate to the stakes
- **Confusing diverge with brainstorm** — diverging means staying open to input that might contradict your assumptions, not just generating more ideas

- Design Council. *The Double Diamond: A Universally Accepted Depiction of the Design Process* (updated 2019) — available at designcouncil.org.uk

### Recommended Books

## Page 3: Issue Trees & MECE
**URL:** `/library/scoping-diagnosis/issue-trees-mece/`
**One-line definition:** A method for decomposing a problem into its component sub-questions in a structure that is Mutually Exclusive and Collectively Exhaustive — so that nothing overlaps and nothing is missing.

### Overview
The MECE principle — **M**utually **E**xclusive, **C**ollectively **E**xhaustive — was developed at McKinsey & Company and is closely associated with **Barbara Minto**, a McKinsey consultant who joined in 1963. Minto formalized MECE as the structural backbone of her **Pyramid Principle**, first published internally at McKinsey and later as a book in 1987 (Pitman Publishing; revised editions under various publishers). The principle applies both to how you structure an argument and how you structure a problem analysis.

The issue tree itself — the visual decomposition of a problem into MECE branches — is a standard tool across management consulting firms, widely taught at McKinsey, Bain, and BCG as part of their analyst training.

Two types:
1. **Diagnostic tree** (the "why" tree): decomposing why a problem is happening — used for root-cause analysis and problem diagnosis. Example: "Why is revenue declining?" → Is it price? Volume? Mix?
2. **Solution tree** (the "how" tree): decomposing the possible ways to address a problem — used for option generation and hypothesis development. Example: "How might we grow revenue?" → New customers? Existing customers spending more? Higher price?

### When to Use It
During the scoping and framing phase, once you've agreed on the problem statement. The issue tree is how you go from "here's the question" to "here's how we'll analyze it." Also useful in the hypothesis-driven approach (see that page) — a hypothesis tree is just an issue tree where each branch is stated as a testable claim.

Less useful when the problem is genuinely exploratory and you need open-ended discovery first (see Double Diamond). Don't force MECE structure on a problem you don't yet understand — get to understand it first.

### How It Works
1. **Write the top-level question** at the root of the tree. Be precise — a vague question produces a vague tree.
2. **Identify 2–5 branches** that collectively answer the root question. Each branch is a sub-question. Test: do the branches together cover the full problem? Do any two branches overlap?
3. **Apply the MECE test** to each level:
   - *Mutually exclusive*: can an item appear in more than one branch? If yes, your categories overlap.
   - *Collectively exhaustive*: could there be a cause or option that doesn't fit any branch? If yes, you have a gap.
4. **Go deeper** — break each branch into its own sub-questions, applying MECE at each level.
5. **Stop when you hit answerable questions** — branches that can be resolved with data, analysis, or a clear hypothesis.
6. **Assign branches** — in a team, give each person or workstream a branch to own.

Common MECE structures:
- **Algebraic splits**: Revenue = Price × Volume; Profit = Revenue − Cost (inherently MECE)
- **Process splits**: by step in a customer journey or operational process
- **Segment splits**: by customer type, geography, product line — just make sure the segments are exhaustive and non-overlapping

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
After the Client brief and problem reframe, give the team 10–15 minutes to draw the issue tree on a whiteboard. The Analyst drives the structure; the Lead Consultant checks for MECE; the Skeptic's explicit job is to find gaps and overlaps. Assign a branch to each team member for the next 30–40 minutes. When the team reconvenes, the structure of the recommendation should map back to the tree — which branches were confirmed, which were eliminated, and what that means for the answer.

Posting the tree visibly throughout the session keeps the team from drifting. It also gives the Client a clear view of the team's logic.

### Common Pitfalls
- **False MECE** — creating categories that look exhaustive but leave gaps (segmenting by "North America / Europe / Asia" and forgetting Latin America and Africa)
- **Overlap** — "cost reduction" and "operational efficiency" are not MECE; one is a subset of the other
- **Over-engineering the tree** — spending 30 minutes perfecting the structure rather than using it; a good-enough tree used quickly beats a perfect tree used late
- **Wrong tree type** — applying a solution tree when you actually need to diagnose why something is happening first
- **Mistaking MECE structure for insight** — the tree is scaffolding; the insight comes from what you find on each branch

### References & Further Reading

- Rasiel, Ethan M. *The McKinsey Way* (1999, McGraw-Hill)
- Conn, Charles and McLean, Robert. *Bulletproof Problem Solving* (2019, Wiley) — the best modern treatment of issue trees and structured problem solving

### Recommended Books

## Page 4: Hypothesis-Driven Approach
**URL:** `/library/scoping-diagnosis/hypothesis-driven/`
**One-line definition:** Forming a provisional answer to the client's question at the start of the engagement, then structuring the analysis to test it — rather than gathering all possible data first and seeing what emerges.

### Overview
The hypothesis-driven approach is the intellectual signature of management consulting — the habit that most clearly distinguishes consulting problem-solving from academic research or journalism.

It is most closely associated with **McKinsey & Company**, where it is a core element of the firm's problem-solving approach. Ethan Rasiel describes it in *The McKinsey Way* (1999, McGraw-Hill): "McKinsey-ites learn early that they are hypothesis-driven. They don't go out and gather data and then analyze it. They form a hypothesis about a client's problem and then set out to test it." Charles Conn and Robert McLean elaborate the methodology in *Bulletproof Problem Solving* (2019, Wiley), which is the most thorough contemporary treatment.

The underlying intellectual model is the scientific method — form a hypothesis, design an experiment to test it, revise based on evidence. The consulting version is compressed and applied to business problems rather than natural phenomena, but the epistemology is the same.

The contrast is "data-first" consulting — gather everything, then analyze, then conclude. Hypothesis-driven work is more efficient (you only gather the data that tests your hypothesis) and often more insightful (forcing an early answer reveals your assumptions and lets others challenge them). The risk is confirmation bias: gathering only the data that confirms what you already think.

### When to Use It
On virtually every analytical consulting engagement — it's less a specific tool than a mode of thinking. Especially powerful when time is short, when the team has relevant industry or problem experience to draw on for the initial hypothesis, or when the client needs to see early momentum.

When to be cautious: in genuinely open-ended discovery work where the problem is so poorly understood that any early hypothesis would be arbitrary. In these cases, a short divergent phase (see Double Diamond) before forming a hypothesis is appropriate. The discipline is knowing when to diverge first and when to hypothesize first.

### How It Works
1. **Form an initial hypothesis**: Based on what you know from the brief, your experience, and pattern recognition — what do you think the answer is? Write it as a clear statement: "We believe [X] is happening because [Y], and that the best path forward is [Z]."
2. **Build a hypothesis tree**: Restate the issue tree (see that page) with each branch expressed as a testable claim rather than an open question. Not "Is the problem on the revenue side?" but "Revenue decline is driven by volume loss in the mid-market segment, not price."
3. **Design the minimum analysis**: For each branch, what is the single piece of evidence that would confirm or kill it? Do that analysis first.
4. **Build the ghost deck**: Draft a storyboard of the final presentation — with the hypothesis as the conclusion — before the analysis is complete. This sounds backward and feels uncomfortable; that's the point. It forces clarity on what you're trying to prove.
5. **Test and revise**: Follow the data. A team that kills its initial hypothesis and finds a better answer is doing good work. A team that contorts the evidence to protect the initial hypothesis has failed at the method.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
Five minutes into the team work phase, stop and force the team to state an initial hypothesis out loud. Write it on the board. Assign the Analyst to structure the work around testing it. At the end of the 60-minute work phase, check: did the analysis test the hypothesis, or did it just gather interesting facts? At the debrief, evaluate whether the team followed the data or defended their initial answer.

The Skeptic's role in a hypothesis-driven session is to play the null hypothesis — to argue the opposite of whatever the team believes and see if it holds. This is not being difficult; it's doing the work.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Hypothesis as decoration** — stating a hypothesis without actually structuring the analysis around it; the work just becomes data collection with a hypothesis written on top afterward
- **Confirmation bias** — building an analysis that can only confirm, not disprove; a good test must have the possibility of a negative result
- **Premature closure** — not revising the hypothesis when the evidence points elsewhere; protecting the initial answer is the most common intellectual failure in consulting
- **No ghost deck** — skipping the storyboard step and assembling the presentation after all the analysis is done; this typically produces a data dump rather than an argument

### References & Further Reading
- Rasiel, Ethan M. *The McKinsey Way* (1999) — Chapter 1 is the clearest brief description of the hypothesis-driven approach
- Conn, Charles and McLean, Robert. *Bulletproof Problem Solving* (2019) — most thorough treatment
- Kahneman, Daniel. *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011, Farrar, Straus and Giroux) — for understanding the cognitive biases that make hypothesis testing hard

## Page 5: Stakeholder Mapping
**URL:** `/library/scoping-diagnosis/stakeholder-mapping/`
**One-line definition:** A structured analysis of everyone who can affect or be affected by an engagement's outcome — their power, interests, and likely stance — used to shape how the consulting team engages and how the recommendation is positioned.

### Overview
**Stakeholder theory** was formalized by **R. Edward Freeman** in *Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach* (1984, Pitman Publishing), where he defined a stakeholder as "any group or individual who can affect or is affected by the achievement of the organization's objectives." Freeman's work was primarily directed at corporate strategy — arguing that firms must account for stakeholders beyond just shareholders — but consulting quickly adopted the underlying idea as a diagnostic tool for understanding the political landscape of any engagement.

The **Power/Interest grid** — the most widely used mapping tool — is attributed to **Aubrey Mendelow**, who presented the concept at the 1981 International Conference of Information Systems. It places stakeholders on a two-by-two matrix:
- **High power, high interest**: Manage closely — these are your primary audience
- **High power, low interest**: Keep satisfied — they can block you; don't give them a reason to
- **Low power, high interest**: Keep informed — they'll amplify your message if it serves their interests
- **Low power, low interest**: Monitor — periodic check-in is sufficient

Beyond the basic grid, experienced consultants also track:
- **Stance** (supportive / neutral / resistant) — often more actionable than power/interest alone
- **Influence channels** (formal authority / informal networks / expertise / close relationships with decision-makers)
- **Stakes** (what does this person lose or gain if the recommendation is adopted?)

### When to Use It
Early in the engagement — ideally as part of the initial scoping — and revisited when the recommendation is being formed. The map is most useful at two moments: (1) when deciding how to structure the analysis and whose input to seek; and (2) when planning how to land the recommendation.

In Consulting Club sessions, stakeholder mapping is useful for understanding the political dimension of the client's problem, even when the presenting question is analytical. Most real business problems have stakeholder dynamics embedded in them.

### How It Works
1. **Generate a full list** — brainstorm everyone who touches this situation. Don't edit yet; the point is to be exhaustive.
2. **Assess power** — who can actually block or champion the recommendation? Consider formal authority, budget control, and informal influence.
3. **Assess interest** — how much does this person care about the outcome? High interest means they're engaged; low interest means they may be unaware or indifferent.
4. **Plot on the grid** — assign each stakeholder to a quadrant.
5. **Add stance** — mark each stakeholder as supportive, neutral, or resistant. This turns the map from descriptive to strategic.
6. **Identify influence relationships** — who influences whom? Sometimes the highest-leverage move is winning over an informal influencer, not the formal decision-maker.
7. **Develop an engagement strategy** — for each quadrant, what do you do? Manage closely means regular updates and direct access; keep satisfied means proactive reassurance; keep informed means clear communication; monitor means lightweight check-ins.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
During the first 20 minutes of team work, have the Scribe build a rough stakeholder map while the Analyst begins framework analysis. The Lead Consultant should use the map to shape the recommendation — specifically: who needs to be convinced for this to actually happen? The recommendation isn't just "what should they do" but "what should they do, and how should they sell it internally."

The best Consulting Club recommendations include a stakeholder dimension: "Here's the answer, and here's who you need to bring along."

### Common Pitfalls
- **Mapping without acting** — producing a beautiful map and then ignoring it; the point is to change how you engage
- **Static thinking** — stakeholder positions shift as the engagement progresses; a resistant stakeholder may become supportive when they understand what's in it for them
- **Forgetting informal power** — the executive assistant who controls calendar access, the technical lead whose buy-in is required for implementation, the former executive whose opinion is still sought — these rarely appear on org charts
- **Conflating who you're mapping for** — sometimes you need to map stakeholders in the client's world (to shape the recommendation); sometimes you need to map stakeholders in your own engagement (to know who to interview)
- **Power without insight** — a high-power, high-interest stakeholder who is resistant is your biggest risk; the map identifies them but doesn't tell you how to move them; that requires a separate strategy

### References & Further Reading
- Freeman, R. Edward. *Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach* (1984, Pitman Publishing)
- Mendelow, Aubrey. "Environmental Scanning: The Impact of the Stakeholder Concept" (1981, ICIS proceedings)

## Page 6: Root-Cause Tools — 5 Whys & Ishikawa / Fishbone
**URL:** `/library/scoping-diagnosis/root-cause-tools/`
**One-line definition:** Two structured techniques for drilling past symptoms to the underlying cause of a problem: the 5 Whys for linear causal chains, and the Ishikawa (fishbone) diagram for problems with multiple possible causes across categories.

### Overview
These two tools emerged from quality management in Japanese manufacturing and have since become standard across consulting, operations, and product work.

**5 Whys** is attributed to **Sakichi Toyoda**, the founder of Toyota Industries, who developed the approach in the early 20th century as part of Toyota's scientific approach to manufacturing problems. It was formalized and championed by **Taiichi Ohno**, architect of the Toyota Production System, who describes it in *Toyota Production System: Beyond Large-Scale Production* (1978; English translation 1988, Productivity Press): "By asking 'why' five times... we can identify the root cause of a defect." The technique was later popularized in Western management through Lean and Six Sigma adoption in the 1990s and 2000s.

**The Ishikawa diagram** (also called the fishbone diagram, or cause-and-effect diagram) was developed by **Kaoru Ishikawa**, a professor at the University of Tokyo, in 1943 while working with engineers at Kawasaki Shipyards. Ishikawa designed it as a quality-control tool to help teams organize hypotheses about the causes of a defect. He published it formally in *Guide to Quality Control* (1968, Japanese; 1976, English, Asian Productivity Organization). The diagram was one of Ishikawa's "Seven Basic Tools of Quality."

The two tools are complementary:
- **5 Whys** is best for linear causal chains: each answer produces the next question in a sequence.
- **Ishikawa** is best for problems where causes are multiple and categorical — it forces a systematic scan across different types of causes rather than following a single chain.

### When to Use Them
When the client's presenting problem is a symptom — something is wrong, but why — and your job is to identify the root cause before recommending a fix. Root-cause tools are most valuable in diagnostic engagements: performance problems, process failures, recurring issues that surface treatments have failed to fix. Less necessary when the cause is already understood and the task is solution development.

Use 5 Whys when the situation feels like a chain of events. Use Ishikawa when the problem might have multiple independent causes in different areas. Often useful to run Ishikawa first (to identify the category of likely causes) and then 5 Whys within the most promising category.

### How They Work

**5 Whys:**
1. State the problem clearly at the top.
2. Ask: "Why is this happening?" Write the answer.
3. Ask: "Why is *that* happening?" Write the answer.
4. Repeat until you reach a cause that is actionable — something you can actually fix — and not just another symptom.
5. The "five" is a rule of thumb, not a rule. Some problems require three iterations; some require seven. Stop when you reach a cause that is (a) specific, (b) within someone's control, and (c) whose removal would prevent the problem from recurring.

**Ishikawa / Fishbone:**
1. Draw a horizontal arrow pointing right — the "spine" of the fish — ending at the "effect" (the problem statement).
2. Add major "bones" branching off the spine — these are the cause categories. For business problems, common categories include: **People, Process, Technology, Data, Strategy, External**. For manufacturing (the original context), the classic "6 Ms" are: Machine, Method, Material, Measurement, Man/People, Mother Nature.
3. For each category, brainstorm contributing causes — add them as smaller branches.
4. Identify the most likely root causes — not all branches are equal. Mark the ones the team believes are primary for further analysis.
5. Validate with data — the fishbone is a hypothesis-generation tool; the hypotheses still need testing.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
After the Client brief, use 10 minutes to apply one of these tools to the client's stated problem. Choose 5 Whys if the situation sounds like a chain of events; choose fishbone if there are clearly several different areas to investigate.

Post the result on the whiteboard and leave it there. At the recommendation stage, the team should be able to point to a specific root cause and say: "Here's what's actually going on, here's why we think so, and here's what follows from that." If the recommendation doesn't connect to the root cause, something is missing.

The Skeptic should challenge whether the team has reached a true root cause or is still at a symptom. "Sales are declining" is not a root cause. "Sales are declining because the mid-market segment has shifted to a competitor offering better post-sale support, which we don't offer" is a root cause.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Stopping too early** — the single most common failure; three whys often produces the obvious symptom, not the cause
- **5 Whys leading to blame** — "Why did the launch fail? Because marketing didn't execute." That's not a root cause; it's a name. The next why ("Why didn't marketing execute?") is where the real analysis begins
- **Fishbone as decoration** — generating a beautiful diagram without using it to generate hypotheses that get tested
- **Confusing correlation and causation** — "Sales dropped the same week we changed the website" is a correlation; the causal link still needs to be established
- **Stopping at one branch** — in complex problems, there may be multiple root causes; the fishbone is designed to find all of them, not just the first

## Page 7: Scoping the Engagement
**URL:** `/library/scoping-diagnosis/scoping-engagement/`
**One-line definition:** The process of agreeing, explicitly, on what the consulting engagement will and will not address — defining objectives, deliverables, constraints, and out-of-scope boundaries before work begins.

### Overview
Scoping is not a single framework with a clear inventor — it is a foundational discipline of professional services, embedded in consulting firm training, project management methodology (the Project Management Institute's *PMBOK Guide* devotes significant coverage to scope management), and professional services best practice.

Two books treat the consulting-specific version most usefully: **Peter Block's *Flawless Consulting*** (1981, third edition 2011, Pfeiffer), which focuses on the contracting conversation between consultant and client; and **Paul Friga's *The McKinsey Engagement*** (2009, McGraw-Hill), which covers McKinsey's practical approach to structuring an engagement from kick-off through delivery.

The core problem scoping addresses is **scope creep** — the gradual, often imperceptible expansion of an engagement beyond its original intent. It happens in small steps: a client says "while you're at it, can you also look at..." and suddenly the team is doing twice the work with the same time and resources. In a 90-minute session, the analog is time creep: the team spends 40 minutes on one branch of the problem and has nothing coherent for the recommendation.

The formal output of a scoping conversation in commercial consulting is a **Statement of Work (SOW)** — a written document specifying what the team will deliver, by when, and what is explicitly excluded. For Consulting Club, the Client Brief Template (see Session Kit) performs the same function.

### When to Use It
At the very start of every engagement — immediately after (or simultaneously with) problem framing. Also when a client adds a new question mid-engagement: pause, evaluate whether the new question is within scope, and explicitly renegotiate if not.

### How It Works
A well-scoped engagement answers five questions:

**1. Objectives** — what is the engagement trying to achieve? Express in terms of outcomes, not activities: not "we will analyze the competitive landscape" but "you will leave with a clear view of which two market segments offer the best growth opportunity."

**2. Deliverables** — what specific outputs will the team produce? A good deliverable is concrete, completable, and verifiable: a slide deck, a recommendation, a prioritized list. Not "insights" — insights are not a deliverable.

**3. Constraints** — what are the real boundaries? Time (90 minutes), access (what data and people are available), and assumptions (what does the team take as given vs. test?).

**4. Out-of-scope** — what will the team explicitly *not* address? This is underused and underrated. Stating what's out of scope does two things: it prevents the team from wandering, and it manages the client's expectations about what they'll receive. "We will not address implementation planning" is not a failure — it's an honest boundary.

**5. Success criteria** — how will the client know if the engagement succeeded? "You'll have a recommendation you can take to your board" is a success criterion. "We'll do good analysis" is not.

**The scoping conversation itself** is as important as the document. Block argues in *Flawless Consulting* that the consultant's job in contracting is to negotiate both the business problem and the relationship: establishing genuine collaboration, not just taking orders. A client who doesn't own the scoping won't own the recommendation.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
During the Client brief, the Scribe should capture a quick scope statement on the board. A useful template:

> *"We are trying to answer: [question]. We will produce: [deliverable]. We will NOT address: [out-of-scope]. We'll know we succeeded if: [success criterion]."*

Take 90 seconds to read it back to the Client before work begins. If the Client says "actually, I also need you to cover X," the Lead Consultant should make a judgment call: is X additive (can be handled in the same analysis) or substitutive (requires a different approach)? If substitutive, renegotiate before proceeding.

This discipline is one of the clearest differentiators between teams that deliver coherent recommendations and teams that deliver interesting-but-unfocused presentations. The teams that get lost usually skipped the scoping step.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Vague objectives** — "help us improve" is not an objective; push for something concrete and evaluable
- **No out-of-scope list** — if everything is potentially in scope, nothing is in scope; the absence of explicit exclusions is an invitation to drift
- **Scope as contract** — real consultants renegotiate scope when they discover the problem is different than expected; treat the scope statement as a working hypothesis, not a binding contract
- **Over-scoping to impress** — promising more than the time allows; better to deliver one sharp recommendation than three half-baked ones
- **Confusing output with outcome** — "a 20-page report" is an output; "a decision you can make with confidence" is an outcome; scope the outcome
