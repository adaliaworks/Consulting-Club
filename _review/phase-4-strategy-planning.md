## Page 1: OKRs
**URL:** `/library/strategy-planning/okrs/`
**One-line definition:** A goal-setting framework that pairs qualitative Objectives with measurable Key Results, used to align organizations around direction and track progress against it on a regular cadence.

### Overview
OKRs trace to **Andy Grove**, the legendary CEO of Intel, who developed the approach in the 1970s as a more disciplined version of Peter Drucker's Management by Objectives (introduced in *The Practice of Management*, 1954). Grove described his methodology in *High Output Management* (1983, Random House) — still one of the best management books ever written. Grove's version emphasized the discipline of separating aspirational direction (the Objective) from measurable outcomes (the Key Results) and running the cycle quarterly rather than annually.

**John Doerr**, a venture capitalist at Kleiner Perkins who had worked under Grove at Intel, introduced OKRs to Google in 1999 when the company had fewer than 40 employees. Google's sustained use — and the success of companies that followed — made OKRs the dominant goal-setting framework in tech and later across industries. Doerr's book *Measure What Matters* (2018, Portfolio/Penguin) brought them to a broader business audience through Google's story and case studies from Intel, Bono's ONE Campaign, the Gates Foundation, and others.

The structure:
- **Objective**: qualitative, inspirational, directional — answers "where are we going?" A good Objective is memorable, motivating, and unambiguous in its direction. Not a number.
- **Key Results**: measurable, time-bound, typically 3–5 per Objective — answers "how will we know we got there?" Key Results are outcomes, not activities. Not "launch the product" but "achieve 10,000 active users by end of quarter."

Key design principles:
- OKRs are typically quarterly (occasionally annual for strategic OKRs)
- Targets are graded 0–1.0 at the close of the period; **0.7 is considered success** — a 1.0 suggests the goal was set too low
- The deliberate stretch: OKRs should be aspirational enough that achieving 70% still represents meaningful progress
- Transparency: OKRs are typically public within an organization, creating alignment and accountability
- Separation from compensation: unlike traditional MBOs, OKRs are designed not to be directly tied to performance reviews, which would incentivize sandbagging

### When to Use It
When a client needs to improve organizational alignment, prioritization, or accountability — particularly in faster-moving organizations where annual planning cycles can't keep up with how the environment changes. OKRs are most powerful when the organization has a clear strategy and needs to translate it into quarterly execution focus. Less useful when the organization hasn't yet agreed on strategic direction — setting OKRs before that is prioritizing before choosing.

### How It Works
1. **Set the Objective** — start with the most important thing the team needs to accomplish this quarter. Write it as a headline: memorable, directional, non-numeric.
2. **Define 3–5 Key Results** — for each KR, ask: "If we achieve this, would we be confident the Objective was met?" KRs should be measurable (have a number), outcomes (not activities), and collectively sufficient to validate the Objective.
3. **Check for sandbagging** — is each KR achievable at 70% effort? If yes, stretch it. If 70% requires a real push, it's calibrated right.
4. **Cascade and connect** — team OKRs should connect to company OKRs; individual OKRs should connect to team OKRs. The connection creates alignment without rigid top-down control.
5. **Check in regularly** — weekly or bi-weekly team check-ins on OKR progress; surface blockers early; update confidence scores. The cadence matters as much as the targets.
6. **Score and learn** — at the end of the quarter, grade each KR, reflect on what drove results and what didn't, and use the learning to set better OKRs next quarter.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
OKRs come up most often when a client's problem is organizational: "We're not executing against our strategy" or "Different teams are pulling in different directions." Use the framework to diagnose whether the misalignment is strategic (unclear direction) or executional (poor goal-setting and tracking). In the recommendation phase, sketch one or two example OKRs for the client's situation to make the advice concrete — it's one thing to say "set better goals," another to show what a well-formed Objective and Key Results look like for their specific problem.

### Common Pitfalls
- **KRs as activities** — "launch the new feature" is an output, not an outcome; real KRs measure results: "increase feature adoption to 40% of active users"
- **Too many OKRs** — organizations that set 10+ OKRs per quarter are prioritizing nothing; 3–5 Objectives with 3–5 KRs each is the upper bound; fewer is usually better
- **Sandbagging** — setting targets that are easily achievable to guarantee a good score; destroys the value of the framework and signals a culture of risk-aversion
- **Tying directly to compensation** — creates powerful incentives to lowball targets; Grove and Doerr both explicitly caution against this
- **Treating OKRs as a reporting tool** — OKRs are for alignment and learning, not status updates; if the weekly check-in becomes a recitation of metrics, the system has been bureaucratized

### References & Further Reading
- Grove, Andrew S. *High Output Management* (1983, Random House)
- Doerr, John. *Measure What Matters: How Google, Bono, and the Gates Foundation Rock the World with OKRs* (2018, Portfolio/Penguin)
- Drucker, Peter F. *The Practice of Management* (1954, Harper & Row) — the MBO precursor

## Page 2: Balanced Scorecard
**URL:** `/library/strategy-planning/balanced-scorecard/`
**One-line definition:** A strategic performance management framework that complements financial measures with three additional perspectives — Customer, Internal Process, and Learning & Growth — to give a more complete picture of organizational health and strategy execution.

### Overview
The Balanced Scorecard was developed by **Robert S. Kaplan** (Harvard Business School) and **David P. Norton** (founder of Nolan Norton Institute), first published in their January–February 1992 *Harvard Business Review* article "The Balanced Scorecard — Measures That Drive Performance." The foundational book, *The Balanced Scorecard: Translating Strategy into Action*, followed in 1996 (Harvard Business School Press) and remains the definitive reference.

The framework emerged from a research study Kaplan and Norton conducted in 1990 studying performance measurement in leading companies. Their central finding: organizations were over-relying on financial measures, which are lagging indicators — they tell you what happened, not what's about to happen. A business could look healthy on financial measures while quietly eroding its competitive position through underinvestment in customers, processes, and people.

The four perspectives:
1. **Financial** — how do we look to shareholders? Revenue growth, profitability, return on capital, cash flow. The outcomes that financial stakeholders care about. Lagging by nature.
2. **Customer** — how do customers see us? Market share, customer satisfaction, customer retention, net promoter score, on-time delivery. Leading indicator for future financials.
3. **Internal Business Process** — what must we excel at? Cycle time, quality, throughput, innovation rate. The processes that create value for customers and ultimately for shareholders.
4. **Learning & Growth** — can we continue to improve and create value? Employee skills, information systems, organizational culture, employee satisfaction. The foundation that enables the other three.

The causal logic runs bottom-up: investments in Learning & Growth improve Internal Processes; better processes drive Customer outcomes; strong customer relationships produce Financial results. The Scorecard makes this causal chain explicit and measures it.

Kaplan and Norton later introduced the **Strategy Map** — a visual representation of cause-and-effect relationships across the four perspectives — in their 2000 HBR article "Having Trouble with Your Strategy? Then Map It." The Strategy Map became as important as the Scorecard itself for communicating strategy.

### When to Use It
When a client needs to translate strategy into measurable execution — particularly large, complex organizations where strategy easily becomes disconnected from day-to-day operations. The Balanced Scorecard is most powerful as an organizational alignment tool: it forces leadership to agree on what matters in each perspective and then holds them accountable to those measures. Also useful for strategy review conversations: looking at performance across all four perspectives together tells a story that financial reports alone don't.

### How It Works
1. **Clarify the strategy** — the Scorecard translates strategy into measures; if the strategy is unclear, the Scorecard will be too. Start with a clear strategic statement or hypothesis.
2. **Develop objectives in each perspective** — for each of the four perspectives, identify 3–5 strategic objectives: specific outcomes the organization needs to achieve to execute the strategy.
3. **Define measures** — for each objective, identify 1–2 measures that indicate progress. Measures should be concrete, measurable, and within the organization's influence.
4. **Set targets** — for each measure, what level of performance would indicate success? Targets should be ambitious but achievable.
5. **Define initiatives** — what specific programs or projects will drive performance against each objective? This is where the Scorecard connects to resource allocation.
6. **Build the Strategy Map** — draw the cause-and-effect relationships across perspectives. Which Learning & Growth objectives drive which Internal Process improvements? Which process improvements drive which Customer outcomes?
7. **Cascade** — develop Scorecards at business unit, department, and team levels that align with the organization-level Scorecard.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
The Balanced Scorecard is most useful in sessions where the client's problem is strategy execution — "we have a strategy but we're not executing against it" or "our metrics are all financial and we can't tell what's coming." Use the four perspectives as a diagnostic structure: which perspective is underperforming? Is the financial problem a symptom of a customer problem, which is a symptom of a process problem, which is rooted in a capability problem? That causal chain often points directly to the root cause and the intervention.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Measure proliferation** — organizations that develop 40+ metrics across the four perspectives have a reporting system, not a strategic management tool; a usable Scorecard has 20–25 measures at most
- **Treating all perspectives equally** — the causal logic runs from Learning & Growth upward; the leading indicators in the bottom two perspectives matter most for strategy
- **Financial dominance by default** — despite the framework's intent, Financial measures often crowd out the others in review conversations; the Skeptic should push back when the discussion drifts entirely to lagging indicators
- **Metrics without owners** — every measure needs a person accountable for it; a Scorecard without ownership is just a dashboard
- **Confusing the Scorecard with a KPI dashboard** — the Balanced Scorecard is a strategic management system built around a theory of how the organization creates value; a KPI dashboard is just a collection of metrics

### References & Further Reading
- Kaplan, Robert S. and Norton, David P. "The Balanced Scorecard — Measures That Drive Performance." *Harvard Business Review* (January–February 1992)
- Kaplan, Robert S. and Norton, David P. *The Balanced Scorecard: Translating Strategy into Action* (1996, Harvard Business School Press)
- Kaplan, Robert S. and Norton, David P. "Having Trouble with Your Strategy? Then Map It." *Harvard Business Review* (September–October 2000)

### Recommended Books

## Page 3: OGSM
**URL:** `/library/strategy-planning/ogsm/`
**One-line definition:** A one-page strategic planning framework that connects Objectives (the destination), Goals (the measurable targets), Strategies (the choices about how to get there), and Measures (the indicators of whether strategies are working).

### Overview
OGSM — Objectives, Goals, Strategies, Measures — is a practical one-page strategy tool with roots in consumer goods planning, most closely associated with **Procter & Gamble**, where it became a standard internal planning format used across the company for decades. The framework's exact origin is contested; it appears in various forms in corporate planning literature from the 1980s and is sometimes attributed to Japanese business planning practices adopted by FMCG companies in the postwar period.

What distinguishes OGSM from other strategy tools is its insistence on a single page. The constraint is the point: it forces the strategic choices that most planning documents bury in slide decks. A well-constructed OGSM communicates the full strategy — where we're going, what success looks like numerically, what we're choosing to do (and not do), and how we'll track it — in a format that any member of the team can read in two minutes.

The four elements:
- **Objective**: the qualitative destination — the strategic ambition stated in clear, memorable terms. One sentence. Answers: "What are we trying to become or achieve?" Not a number.
- **Goals**: the quantitative targets that define what success looks like in a specific time horizon. Typically 2–4 measurable outcomes. Answers: "How will we know we got there?"
- **Strategies**: the choices about *how* the organization will achieve the Goals — what it will do and, crucially, what it will not do. This is the heart of the OGSM; vague strategies produce vague execution. Each strategy should reflect a real choice.
- **Measures**: the leading and lagging indicators the team will track to determine whether each strategy is working. Answers: "Are we on track?" Measures are strategy-level, not just outcome-level.

The OGSM differs from OKRs in its explicit emphasis on strategies as choices, and from the Balanced Scorecard in its simplicity and single-page discipline. It is less a measurement system than a planning and communication tool.

### When to Use It
When a client needs a strategy that the whole organization can actually understand, remember, and act on — particularly in organizations where lengthy strategy documents are written, filed, and forgotten. Also useful for team-level or function-level strategy (a marketing team's OGSM, a regional business unit's OGSM) where the broader corporate strategy is set but execution alignment is weak.

### How It Works
Constructing an OGSM is a series of forcing questions:

1. **Objective first** — write one sentence that captures the strategic destination. If the team can't agree on one sentence, they haven't agreed on the strategy. This is where OGSM earns its value as a conversation tool.
2. **Goals next** — translate the Objective into 2–4 measurable outcomes. Each Goal should be: specific, time-bound, and directly connected to the Objective. If achieving all the Goals wouldn't validate the Objective, something is missing.
3. **Strategies as choices** — for each Goal, ask: "What must we choose to do (and not do) to achieve this?" Good strategies have a "not" embedded in them — "invest in premium positioning, not mass-market penetration" is a strategy; "improve our marketing" is not.
4. **Measures for each strategy** — for each Strategy, identify one or two indicators that signal whether it's working. These are often process measures or leading indicators, not just the Goal outcomes.
5. **Fit it on one page** — if it doesn't fit, the strategy isn't clear enough yet. The discipline of the page constraint forces clarity.
6. **Validate coherence** — does each Strategy connect to a Goal? Does each Goal connect to the Objective? Does achieving all the Strategies make achieving the Goals plausible? Read it as a logic chain, not a list.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
Use OGSM when the client's problem is about strategic clarity or organizational alignment — "we have a lot of priorities and no one can tell which matters most" or "each team is executing against a different version of the strategy." An OGSM sketch produced in the session is a powerful deliverable: it forces the team to make the choices the client has been avoiding, and it produces something the client can take back and use.

The Scribe should draft the OGSM structure on the board early in the team work phase. The Skeptic's job is to challenge every Strategy that doesn't represent a real choice.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Objectives that aren't chosen** — "be the best company in our space" is not a strategic objective; it doesn't tell anyone what to do
- **Strategies that are activities** — "run a digital marketing campaign" is an activity; "win millennial customers through digital-first acquisition" is a strategy
- **Missing the "not"** — if every plausible approach is listed as a Strategy, no choice has been made; real strategy means saying no
- **Measures that duplicate Goals** — measures should track *strategy execution*, not just restate the Goal outcomes; if the only measure is the Goal, the team can't tell whether strategies are working until it's too late
- **Violating the one-page constraint** — the moment the OGSM becomes a multi-page document, its purpose has been lost

### References & Further Reading
- Hanlon, Annmarie. *OGSM: Your One-Page Business Strategy* (2023, Kogan Page)
- The OGSM format is widely practiced at P&G and its alumni network; the best descriptions appear in practitioner articles and marketing strategy textbooks

### Recommended Books
- *OGSM: Your One-Page Business Strategy* — Annmarie Hanlon — `https://www.amazon.com/s?k=ogsm+your+one+page+business+strategy+hanlon&tag=tmiller0e-20`

## Page 4: Blue Ocean Strategy
**URL:** `/library/strategy-planning/blue-ocean-strategy/`
**One-line definition:** A strategic framework for creating uncontested market space — "blue oceans" — by pursuing value innovation rather than competing in existing markets where rivalry is intense and margins are compressed.

### Overview
Blue Ocean Strategy was developed by **W. Chan Kim** and **Renée Mauborgne**, professors at **INSEAD**, first published as an article in *Harvard Business Review* in October 2004 and expanded in their book *Blue Ocean Strategy: How to Create Uncontested Market Space and Make the Competition Irrelevant* (2005, Harvard Business School Press; expanded edition 2015). A follow-up, *Blue Ocean Shift* (2017), provides more practical guidance on implementation.

The core distinction: **red oceans** are existing industries where competitors are fighting for the same customers, margins are compressed, and the water is bloody. **Blue oceans** are new, uncontested market spaces — either entirely new industries (rare) or new pockets of demand within or adjacent to existing industries (more common) — where the rules of competition haven't been set and the opportunity for profitable growth is high.

The strategic logic is **value innovation**: simultaneously pursuing differentiation *and* low cost, rather than accepting the traditional trade-off between them. Value innovation creates a leap in value for buyers while also reducing costs, making the competitive comparison moot rather than fighting it.

Key analytical tools:

**The Strategy Canvas**: a line graph plotting how an industry's players perform on the key factors of competition (price, product features, service, etc.). A company's "strategic profile" (its value curve) shows how it competes along these dimensions. Blue Ocean Strategies produce value curves that look distinctively different from the industry standard.

**The Four Actions Framework**: a systematic way to create a new value curve by asking:
- *Eliminate*: which factors that the industry takes for granted should be eliminated entirely?
- *Reduce*: which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard?
- *Raise*: which factors should be raised well above the industry standard?
- *Create*: which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?

Classic examples Kim and Mauborgne use: Cirque du Soleil (eliminated animals and star performers from circus, created theatrical experience), Southwest Airlines (eliminated meals and hub connections, raised frequency and friendliness), [yellow tail] wine (eliminated complexity and prestige, created easy approachability).

### When to Use It
When a client is facing intense competitive pressure in a mature market and is looking for a way to escape rather than fight harder. Also valuable for product strategy, market entry, or innovation sessions where the client is stuck thinking about the existing competitive frame. Blue Ocean is most useful as a generative tool — it helps teams see new possibilities by systematically questioning what the industry takes for granted.

### How It Works
1. **Draw the current Strategy Canvas** — identify the key factors of competition in the industry; plot the industry average and the client's current profile.
2. **Identify where value is concentrated and where it's wasted** — which factors are expensive but not valued by the target customer? Which are underinvested but matter a lot?
3. **Apply the Four Actions Framework** — for each factor: Eliminate? Reduce? Raise? Create? Force genuine choices; do not just raise everything.
4. **Define the target non-customer** — Blue Ocean strategies often succeed by serving people who are *not* current customers of the industry. Who is being excluded by the current industry model? What would it take to bring them in?
5. **Draw the new value curve** — what does the client's profile look like after applying the Four Actions? Does it diverge meaningfully from the industry standard?
6. **Test for viability** — does the new model produce sufficient value that customers would switch (or choose over alternatives)? Does the cost structure work at the required price point?

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
Blue Ocean is best used as a generative lens, not an analytical one — it opens up possibility space. Use the Four Actions Framework in the development phase when the team is generating strategic options. The Skeptic's job is to push back on Eliminate and Reduce: these are the moves most teams resist, but they're often where the real innovation and cost advantage come from. The deliverable is usually a sketched Strategy Canvas showing the current state and a proposed future value curve, with the strategic logic explained.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Misidentifying blue vs. red** — every market eventually turns red; a blue ocean today is red in five years; the framework is about how to find and create new space, not a permanent state
- **Raising everything** — the power of the Four Actions Framework comes from the Eliminate and Reduce moves; a "blue ocean strategy" that only raises and creates is just expensive differentiation
- **Ignoring execution** — the book is better at generating strategy options than at implementation; a brilliant value canvas that the organization can't build is not a strategy
- **Misusing the examples** — Cirque du Soleil and Southwest are compelling but unusual; most applications are more modest; don't oversell the transformation

### References & Further Reading
- Kim, W. Chan and Mauborgne, Renée. "Blue Ocean Strategy." *Harvard Business Review* (October 2004)
- Kim, W. Chan and Mauborgne, Renée. *Blue Ocean Strategy* (2005; expanded edition 2015, Harvard Business School Press)
- Kim, W. Chan and Mauborgne, Renée. *Blue Ocean Shift* (2017, Hachette Books)

### Recommended Books
- *Blue Ocean Strategy* — Kim & Mauborgne — `https://www.amazon.com/s?k=blue+ocean+strategy+kim+mauborgne&tag=tmiller0e-20`
- *Blue Ocean Shift* — Kim & Mauborgne — `https://www.amazon.com/s?k=blue+ocean+shift+kim+mauborgne&tag=tmiller0e-20`

## Page 5: Three Horizons
**URL:** `/library/strategy-planning/three-horizons/`
**One-line definition:** A framework for managing a portfolio of strategic initiatives across three time horizons — defending the core business today, building emerging growth businesses for tomorrow, and creating the options for the future.

### Overview
The Three Horizons framework was developed at **McKinsey & Company** by **Mehrdad Baghai**, **Stephen Coley**, and **David White**, first published in their book *The Alchemy of Growth: Practical Insights for Building the Enduring Enterprise* (2000, Perseus Books). It emerged from a multi-year McKinsey research project studying how companies sustain profitable growth over long periods — a rarer achievement than it appears.

Their finding: companies that maintain long-term growth do so by actively managing three distinct types of business simultaneously, not by sequentially moving from one to the next.

The three horizons:
- **Horizon 1 — Defend and extend the core**: the existing business that generates today's revenue and profit. Management focus is on operational excellence, customer retention, and incremental improvement. Time horizon: now through 1–2 years. The risk: over-investment that starves the other horizons; or under-investment that erodes the foundation.
- **Horizon 2 — Build emerging businesses**: ventures that are past the idea stage and showing early commercial promise, but not yet generating significant profit. These are tomorrow's core businesses. Management focus is on scaling — finding the right model before the window closes. Time horizon: 2–5 years. The risk: they get starved of attention because they're neither as urgent as H1 nor as exciting as H3.
- **Horizon 3 — Create viable options**: early-stage ideas, experiments, and explorations that could become the foundation of the business in 5+ years. Management focus is on options creation — running low-cost experiments across multiple directions. Time horizon: 5+ years. The risk: underinvestment in genuinely new possibilities because they're speculative and distant.

The key managerial insight: **all three horizons must be actively managed simultaneously**. Companies that focus only on H1 are optimizing for the present at the expense of the future; they may be highly efficient as they slowly become irrelevant. Companies that jump to H3 without a profitable H1 to fund it rarely survive long enough to see the future arrive.

### When to Use It
When a client is struggling with the tension between short-term performance and long-term investment — "we can't afford to invest in new things while we're fighting fires in the core" or "we keep starting new initiatives but never see them through." Also useful for portfolio allocation conversations: where is the client's investment concentrated, and is that appropriate given the maturity of the core business?

### How It Works
1. **Map the portfolio** — for each major initiative, business unit, or investment, assign it to a horizon. Be honest: what stage is each genuinely in?
2. **Assess the balance** — is investment appropriately distributed across all three? A company with everything in H1 and nothing in H2 or H3 is under-investing in the future; a company with everything in H3 hasn't built the foundation.
3. **Identify the H2 gap** — H2 is the most commonly neglected horizon; companies love the security of H1 and the excitement of H3 but under-resource the messy, uncertain work of scaling an emerging business.
4. **Set different management approaches** — each horizon requires different talent, different metrics, different tolerance for failure. A P&L approach appropriate for H1 kills H3 initiatives. Explicitly design different operating models for each.
5. **Define the transition conditions** — what would cause an H3 idea to be promoted to H2? What would cause an H2 venture to be promoted to H1 (or killed)? Clear criteria prevent both premature scaling and endless incubation.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
Three Horizons surfaces most naturally in sessions about growth strategy, innovation, or organizational tension between exploitation and exploration. Use it to diagnose where the client's portfolio is concentrated, and whether that's appropriate. The most common finding is an H2 gap: the client has a strong core (H1) and some exciting ideas (H3) but nothing between them that's actually being resourced to scale. That finding usually changes the conversation about where to invest next.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Treating horizons as sequential** — "we'll focus on H1 until it's stable, then move to H2" is how companies fall behind; the horizons must be managed simultaneously
- **Under-resourcing H2** — the most common failure; H2 businesses require dedicated resources and management attention, not the spare capacity left over after H1 is served
- **Misclassifying by time** — horizons describe management focus and investment stage, not just time horizon; a 2-year project run with H3 operating principles is still an H3 project
- **Using Three Horizons to justify everything** — saying every initiative is strategically important because it fits "one of the horizons" defeats the purpose; the framework's value is in forcing prioritization within each horizon

### References & Further Reading
- Baghai, Mehrdad; Coley, Stephen; White, David. *The Alchemy of Growth: Practical Insights for Building the Enduring Enterprise* (2000, Perseus Books)
- Christensen, Clayton M. *The Innovator's Dilemma* (1997, Harvard Business School Press) — the foundational work on why successful companies fail to invest in disruptive horizons

### Recommended Books
- *The Alchemy of Growth* — Baghai, Coley & White — `https://www.amazon.com/s?k=the+alchemy+of+growth+baghai&tag=tmiller0e-20`

## Page 6: Scenario Planning
**URL:** `/library/strategy-planning/scenario-planning/`
**One-line definition:** A planning discipline that builds multiple plausible stories about how the future might unfold — not to predict which will occur, but to develop strategies that are robust across multiple possibilities.

### Overview
Scenario planning was developed as a formal business discipline at **Royal Dutch Shell** in the early 1970s, primarily through the work of **Pierre Wack**, head of Group Planning. Wack's team developed scenarios that anticipated the 1973 OPEC oil embargo — the only major oil company to have done so — and used them to position Shell for the energy shock while competitors were caught flat-footed. Wack described the methodology in two landmark *Harvard Business Review* articles: "Scenarios: Uncharted Waters Ahead" (September–October 1985) and "Scenarios: Shooting the Rapids" (November–December 1985).

The intellectual roots of scenario work run deeper. **Herman Kahn** at the RAND Corporation developed scenario-based thinking for military planning in the 1950s and 1960s, coining the phrase "thinking the unthinkable." Kahn later applied these methods at the Hudson Institute to social and technological forecasting.

**Peter Schwartz**, who worked at Shell under Wack and later co-founded the **Global Business Network (GBN)**, brought scenario planning to a broader audience through his book *The Art of the Long View* (1991, Currency/Doubleday) — the most accessible introduction to the methodology. **Kees van der Heijden's** *Scenarios: The Art of Strategic Conversation* (1996, Wiley) provides the most rigorous practitioner's framework.

The essence of the method: instead of a single forecast (which will be wrong), build 2–4 internally consistent stories about different ways the future might unfold, based on the interaction of critical uncertainties. Then develop strategies that are robust across multiple scenarios, and identify early warning indicators that signal which scenario is developing.

### When to Use It
When the future is genuinely uncertain in ways that matter strategically — technology disruption, regulatory change, geopolitical shifts, macroeconomic scenarios. Scenario planning is most valuable for: long-range strategy development (5–10 year horizons), stress-testing existing strategies against adverse futures, and capital allocation decisions with long payback periods. Less useful for near-term operational planning where uncertainty is relatively low and execution speed matters more than optionality.

### How It Works
The classic four-step approach:

1. **Identify the focal question** — what strategic decision or challenge is the scenario exercise designed to inform? Good scenario planning is always anchored to a decision, not done as a general futuring exercise.

2. **Identify key driving forces and critical uncertainties** — brainstorm the macro forces that will shape the future for this decision (use PESTEL as a starting point). Then separate what is *predetermined* (trends that will occur regardless — population aging, internet penetration) from what is *critically uncertain* (things that could go multiple ways and would have high impact if they did — regulatory direction, technology adoption rate, geopolitical stability).

3. **Build the scenarios** — select the two most critical and orthogonal uncertainties. Place them on two axes to create a 2×2 matrix. Each quadrant is a scenario — a world defined by a particular combination of how those two uncertainties resolve. Give each scenario a memorable name and write a compelling story for it: what happened, who the winners and losers were, what a company in that world would face.

4. **Develop strategic implications** — for each scenario, ask: what would be the right strategy if this future unfolds? What would we regret if we hadn't done? What options should we create now that would be valuable across multiple scenarios? Identify *robust strategies* (good across all scenarios), *hedging strategies* (reduce downside in specific scenarios), and *early warning signals* (indicators that tell you which scenario is developing).

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
Scenario planning in a 90-minute session is necessarily abbreviated — but even a compressed version is valuable. Use the first 20 minutes to identify 2 critical uncertainties (not more) and sketch 2–4 scenarios. Then focus the remaining time on the strategic implications: which strategy looks good in all four? Which looks catastrophically bad in one? The deliverable is not a polished scenario set but a strategic stress-test: "Your current plan assumes [scenario X]. Here's what it looks like under [scenario Y] — and here's the strategic hedge worth considering."

### Common Pitfalls
- **Scenarios as forecasts** — the most common misuse; scenarios are *not* predictions of what will happen, they're structured thought experiments; the moment a team starts arguing about which scenario is "most likely," they've lost the method
- **Too many scenarios** — more than four scenarios is usually unmanageable; two to four is the right range
- **Scenarios that all look the same** — if the scenarios feel like minor variations on a theme, the critical uncertainties weren't truly orthogonal; find genuinely different axes
- **No decision connection** — scenarios divorced from a specific strategic question produce interesting intellectual exercises, not strategic insight; always anchor to the decision
- **Skipping the early warning signals** — the most actionable output of scenario work is often the list of indicators that tell you which future is developing; teams that skip this step leave the most practical value on the table

## Page 7: Wardley Mapping
**URL:** `/library/strategy-planning/wardley-mapping/`
**One-line definition:** A visual strategy tool that maps the components of a value chain against their stage of evolution — helping organizations identify where to invest, where to commoditize, and where competitive advantage can actually be built.

### Overview
Wardley Mapping was developed by **Simon Wardley** while he was CEO of Fotango, a Canon subsidiary, in the early 2000s. Wardley found himself unable to communicate clearly about strategy without understanding the competitive landscape his organization was operating in — and unable to find a tool that combined business model, competitive position, and the evolutionary dynamics of markets in a single view. He developed the mapping method out of necessity and later documented it extensively in a series of blog posts (available at medium.com/@swardley and in book form) and in *Wardley Maps* (2020, self-published under Creative Commons).

The core insight: **components evolve**. Everything in a value chain — technology, practices, data, knowledge — moves over time through predictable stages from novel to commodity. Most strategy frameworks treat components as static; Wardley Maps treat them as dynamic, with profound implications for where and how to compete.

The two axes:
- **Y-axis (Value Chain)**: vertical position represents visibility to the user — components at the top are directly visible and valuable to the customer; components at the bottom are infrastructure that the customer doesn't see but that the top depends on.
- **X-axis (Evolution)**: horizontal position represents the evolutionary stage of the component, from left to right: **Genesis** (novel, uncertain, rare) → **Custom-built** (bespoke, specific to a few organizations) → **Product** (increasingly standardized, sold off-the-shelf) → **Commodity/Utility** (standardized, widely available, often consumed as a service).

The strategic implications of evolution:
- **Genesis** components are sources of genuine competitive differentiation — and are expensive and uncertain to manage
- **Custom-built** components offer competitive advantage but require internal expertise and are transitioning toward product
- **Product** components should typically be bought, not built — building what can be bought cheaply is waste
- **Commodity/Utility** components should be outsourced or consumed as services — Amazon Web Services is compute at commodity stage; spending engineering effort here is strategic malpractice

Wardley Maps reveal strategic opportunities: components that are still at Custom-built or Product stage but are close to commoditization represent opportunities to standardize or outsource; components that are still at Genesis in your competitors' maps but are moving toward Product in yours represent potential competitive advantages.

### When to Use It
When the client's question involves technology strategy, build vs. buy vs. partner decisions, competitive positioning in dynamic markets, or organizational design in tech-intensive industries. Particularly powerful for technology companies, digital transformations, and any strategy conversation where the pace of change in underlying components matters.

### How It Works
1. **Identify user needs** — start with what the customer or user actually needs; place these at the top of the map.
2. **Map the value chain** — identify the components required to meet those needs, and the dependencies between them. What does each component depend on? Build down from user needs to underlying enablers.
3. **Assess evolutionary stage** — for each component, estimate where it sits on the evolution axis. Ask: is this available as a commodity/utility? As a product? Or does it require custom development? Is it genuinely novel?
4. **Identify strategic patterns** — where are there opportunities to commoditize (use what others have built)? Where does the map show components that are valuable *and* at Genesis or early Custom-built stages — these are your actual differentiators?
5. **Draw strategic moves** — which components should you invest in to push to the right (commoditize)? Which should you keep at Custom-built because they're genuinely differentiating? Which should you consume from the market rather than build?
6. **Check for inertia** — where is the organization investing in components that are already at Product or Commodity stage? This is where strategic waste is hiding.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
Wardley Maps take longer to build well than most 90-minute sessions allow, but even a partial map can reframe a strategy conversation. Use the evolution axis as a quick diagnostic: have the team list the client's key investments and ask "where on the genesis-to-commodity spectrum does each one sit?" Components the client is treating as differentiators that are actually already products or commodities are often the source of wasted investment and missed competitive opportunity.

The Skeptic should push on any component described as a competitive differentiator: "Is this actually differentiating — or are we building what we could be buying?"

### Common Pitfalls
- **Mapping for completeness rather than insight** — Wardley Maps can become exhaustively detailed; the value is in what the map reveals about strategic choices, not in the completeness of the inventory
- **Ignoring evolution dynamics** — the static picture is less valuable than asking "how fast is this component evolving, and what does that imply for our investment decisions?"
- **Treating all Genesis components as worth investing in** — being early on the evolution curve is valuable only if the component connects to user needs high on the value chain; low-value Genesis work is still low-value
- **Applying it to stable, slow-moving industries** — Wardley Mapping is most valuable in dynamic, technology-intensive markets; in slow-moving industries, the evolution axis matters less

## Page 8: Business Model Canvas
**URL:** `/library/strategy-planning/business-model-canvas/`
**One-line definition:** A one-page visual framework for describing, designing, and analyzing how a business creates, delivers, and captures value — organized across nine building blocks that together form a complete business model.

### Overview
The Business Model Canvas was developed by **Alexander Osterwalder** as part of his PhD research at HEC Lausanne (2004), supervised by **Yves Pigneur**. The framework was published as a book — *Business Model Generation: A Handbook for Visionaries, Game Changers, and Challengers* (2010, Wiley) — co-created with Pigneur and a community of 470 practitioners from 45 countries, making it itself a model of the kind of collaborative, iterative design the book advocates.

The book became one of the best-selling business books of the 2010s. The Canvas became the default tool for startup pitches, corporate innovation teams, and business school curricula because it makes business model design visual, discussable, and iterative in a way that traditional business plans do not.

The nine building blocks, arranged on the one-page canvas:

**Right side — Value delivery:**
- **Customer Segments**: who are you serving? Which customers matter most?
- **Value Propositions**: what problem are you solving or need are you serving for each segment?
- **Channels**: how do you reach customers and deliver the value proposition?
- **Customer Relationships**: what type of relationship does each segment expect?
- **Revenue Streams**: for what value is each segment willing to pay, and how?

**Left side — Value creation:**
- **Key Resources**: what assets are required to deliver the value proposition?
- **Key Activities**: what critical activities must be performed?
- **Key Partnerships**: which activities and resources are sourced from partners?
- **Cost Structure**: what are the most significant costs in the business model?

The canvas is deliberately arranged so that the right side represents the customer-facing elements (where value is delivered) and the left side represents the operational elements (where value is created). The Value Proposition sits at the center, connecting both sides.

The fitness test for a completed Canvas: do all nine blocks form a coherent whole? Does the cost structure support the value proposition? Do the revenue streams reflect what customers actually value? Are the key resources and activities truly aligned to delivering the value proposition — or are they legacies of a different business model?

**Lean Canvas**: Ash Maurya's 2010 adaptation for startups replaces several blocks (Key Partners, Key Activities, Key Resources, Customer Relationships) with Problem, Solution, Key Metrics, and Unfair Advantage — useful for pre-product stages where the emphasis is on validating the business hypothesis rather than designing the operating model.

### When to Use It
When a client is designing a new business, a new product line, or a new go-to-market approach; or when analyzing how an existing business creates value and where the model has weaknesses or unexploited opportunities. The Canvas is particularly powerful in competitive analysis: drawing the Canvas for a competitor (or for how a disruptor's model differs from the incumbent's) often reveals the strategic logic of competitive threats more clearly than conventional market analysis.

### How It Works
1. **Choose the subject** — are you mapping an existing business (diagnostic) or designing a new one (generative)? Be clear about which mode you're in; they require different levels of certainty about each block.
2. **Start with Customer Segments and Value Propositions** — the right side of the Canvas is the foundation; if you don't know who you're serving and what you're offering them, the rest is speculation.
3. **Work outward** — define Channels, Customer Relationships, and Revenue Streams for each Segment/Value Proposition pair.
4. **Then define the left side** — what Resources, Activities, and Partnerships are required to deliver on the right side? Work backward from the value proposition, not forward from your existing capabilities.
5. **Define the Cost Structure** — what are the major cost drivers? Is the model cost-driven (competing on low cost) or value-driven (competing on premium positioning)?
6. **Check for fit** — does the Canvas hang together? Are the Revenue Streams sufficient to support the Cost Structure at realistic scale? Does each Key Activity genuinely connect to delivering the Value Proposition? Are there Key Resources listed that don't appear in any Activity?
7. **Identify vulnerabilities** — which blocks are weakest? Which assumptions are least validated? In a startup, these are the hypotheses to test first; in an established business, these are the strategic risks.

### Running It in a Consulting Club Session
The Business Model Canvas is most useful in sessions where the client is designing something new, evaluating a pivot, or trying to understand why a competitor is winning. Sketching the Canvas on the whiteboard — with the nine blocks clearly marked — gives the team a shared language and a visual anchor for the analysis.

For competitive analysis: draw the incumbent's Canvas and the disruptor's Canvas side by side. The differences almost always tell the strategic story. The blocks where they diverge most are where the competitive advantage or vulnerability lives.

### Common Pitfalls
- **Filling in every block with something** — the Canvas is only as useful as its honesty; "TBD" or "uncertain" in a block is more valuable than a confident-sounding answer that's actually a guess
- **Treating it as a static artifact** — business models evolve; the Canvas is a snapshot and a conversation tool, not a planning document to be filed
- **Left-side bias** — teams with operational or engineering backgrounds often fill in the left side first (because they know what they can build); the Canvas should always start from the customer-facing right side
- **Ignoring interdependencies** — changing one block almost always affects others; a new Revenue Stream may require new Channels; new Key Activities may require new Key Resources; read the Canvas as a system
- **Confusing activity with value** — "provide excellent service" is not a Value Proposition; the Value Proposition describes the specific outcome the customer cares about, not the activity the firm performs
