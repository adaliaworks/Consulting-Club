## RACI
**URL:** `/library/execution-change/raci/`
**Definition:** A responsibility assignment matrix that clarifies who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed for each task or decision — eliminating the ambiguity that causes execution to break down.

**Attribution:** General project management practice embedded in PMI methodology and organizational design literature. RACI as a specific acronym has been in use since the 1980s; no single inventor is reliably identified. The Responsibility Assignment Matrix (RAM) concept appears in systems engineering literature from the 1950s–60s.

**Core mechanics:**
- **R — Responsible**: does the work; can be multiple people
- **A — Accountable**: ultimately answerable for the outcome; must be exactly ONE person per task
- **C — Consulted**: provides input before the work is done; two-way communication
- **I — Informed**: kept in the loop after decisions are made; one-way communication

**Key insight:** the A/R distinction is the most important and most commonly confused. Having two Accountable people means neither is. The R can be shared; the A cannot.

## Kotter's 8 Steps & ADKAR
**URL:** `/library/execution-change/kotters-8-steps/`
**Definition:** Two complementary change management frameworks — Kotter's 8 Steps operating at the organizational level (what does the leadership team do?) and ADKAR operating at the individual level (what does each person need to move through the change?).

**Attribution:**
- **Kotter:** John P. Kotter, Konosuke Matsushita Professor of Leadership at Harvard Business School. First published as "Leading Change: Why Transformation Efforts Fail," *HBR* March–April 1995; book *Leading Change* (1996, Harvard Business School Press).
- **ADKAR:** Developed by Jeff Hiatt, founder of Prosci (a change management research and training firm). Published in *ADKAR: A Model for Change in Business, Government and our Community* (2006, Prosci Learning Center Publications).

**Kotter's 8 Steps:**
1. Create a sense of urgency
2. Build a guiding coalition
3. Form a strategic vision and initiatives
4. Enlist a volunteer army
5. Enable action by removing barriers
6. Generate short-term wins
7. Sustain acceleration
8. Institute change

**ADKAR:**
- Awareness (of the need for change)
- Desire (to support and participate in the change)
- Knowledge (of how to change)
- Ability (to demonstrate required skills and behaviors)
- Reinforcement (to sustain the change)

## Lean / Value-Stream Mapping & Six Sigma (DMAIC)
**URL:** `/library/execution-change/lean-six-sigma/`
**Definition:** Two systematic process improvement methodologies — Lean (eliminating waste and accelerating flow) and Six Sigma / DMAIC (reducing variation and defects through statistical rigor) — often combined as Lean Six Sigma.

**Attribution:**
- **Lean:** Toyota Production System (Taiichi Ohno); "Lean" terminology coined by John Krafcik (1988 MIT paper); popularized by Womack, Jones & Roos in *The Machine That Changed the World* (1990).
- **Value Stream Mapping:** developed at Toyota; brought to Western audiences by Mike Rother & John Shook in *Learning to See* (1998, Lean Enterprise Institute).
- **Six Sigma:** developed at Motorola in 1986, credited to Bill Smith (engineer); championed by CEO Bob Galvin; codified by Mikel Harry. Made famous at GE under Jack Welch in the 1990s.
- **DMAIC:** the Six Sigma problem-solving cycle: Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control.

## Prioritization
**URL:** `/library/execution-change/prioritization/`
**Definition:** A set of tools for making defensible decisions about which initiatives to pursue when resources are limited — the Impact/Effort Matrix, MoSCoW method, and Weighted Scoring each suited to different contexts.

**Attribution:**
- **Impact/Effort Matrix:** No single inventor; widely used in product management and consulting since the 1990s.
- **MoSCoW:** Developed by Dai Clegg of Oracle for the DSDM agile framework, circa 1994. Must have / Should have / Could have / Won't have this time.
- **Weighted Scoring:** Decision analysis technique with roots in operations research; Kepner-Tregoe Decision Analysis (1965) is an early formal version.
