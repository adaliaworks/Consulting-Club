## The Pyramid Principle
**URL:** `/library/synthesis-communication/pyramid-principle/`
**Definition:** A communication framework that structures any argument so the answer — the main point — comes first, supported by grouped and summarized reasoning, so the reader or listener never gets lost and never has to wait for the point.

**Attribution:** Barbara Minto, McKinsey & Company consultant (joined 1963). First published internally at McKinsey; published externally as *The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking* (1987, Pitman; revised editions Pearson/FT Press).

**Core mechanics:**
- Answer first (BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front): the recommendation or key message leads
- Group and summarize: every grouping of supporting points should be summarized in a single governing thought
- SCQA structure for framing: Situation → Complication → Question → Answer
- Two argument structures: Deductive (premise → implication → conclusion) and Inductive (parallel points that share a category)

## Executive-Summary Discipline
**URL:** `/library/synthesis-communication/executive-summary/`
**Definition:** The discipline of distilling a complex analysis into a single page or slide that contains only what a senior decision-maker needs to act — situation, complication, recommendation, and the minimum logic required to accept it.

**Attribution:** No single originator — a communication discipline embedded in consulting firm training and executive communication best practices. Cross-references Barbara Minto's BLUF principle; also informed by Jeff Bezos's "six-pager" memo culture at Amazon and military BLUF communication standards.

## Storyboarding the Deck
**URL:** `/library/synthesis-communication/storyboarding/`
**Definition:** The practice of building a presentation's argumentative structure — its "story" — before slides are created, ensuring that the logic holds as a sequence and each page earns its place.

**Attribution:** Storyboarding as a technique was developed at Walt Disney Studios in the early 1930s (generally attributed to Webb Smith, circa 1933). Its application to business presentations was embedded in consulting firm training — the "ghost deck" practice is closely associated with McKinsey's hypothesis-driven approach (see Hypothesis-Driven Approach page).

**Core concepts:**
- Horizontal logic: the sequence of slide headlines, read alone, tells the complete story
- Vertical logic: each slide's body content supports and only supports its headline
- The ghost deck: sketch the full storyboard before any analysis is run
- The "so what on every slide" rule

## The "So What?" Test
**URL:** `/library/synthesis-communication/so-what-test/`
**Definition:** A synthesis discipline that distinguishes observation from insight by demanding that every finding answer the question "so what?" — forcing the analyst to complete the logic chain between data and implication.

**Attribution:** Not attributed to a single inventor — a core discipline of consulting synthesis embedded in McKinsey training and described in Ethan Rasiel's *The McKinsey Way* (1999) and Paul Friga's *The McKinsey Engagement* (2009). Connected to Barbara Minto's Pyramid Principle: the "so what?" question is how you climb the pyramid from supporting facts to governing thought.

**The insight hierarchy:**
- **Data:** "Revenue declined 12% in Q3."
- **Observation:** "Revenue declined 12% in Q3, concentrated in the mid-market segment."
- **Insight:** "Revenue declined 12% in Q3 in mid-market because customers are choosing the competitor's self-serve option for smaller deals — which means our enterprise-only sales model is structurally misaligned to this segment."

The "so what?" test is applied at every level: so what about that data point? So what about that trend? So what about this root cause? Each answer is the next level up.
