## Client Brief Template
**URL:** `/library/session-kit/client-brief-template/`

A fill-in form the Client completes (or works through verbally) to open the session. The goal is a clean, shared problem statement before the Consulting Team begins work.

**Fields:**
1. **Your name and role** — who you are and where you sit
2. **The situation** — 3–5 sentences of context: what's going on, what's changed, why it matters now
3. **The core question** — one sentence: what do you actually need the team to help you answer?
4. **What you've already tried** — what approaches, analyses, or solutions have already been explored (and why they didn't fully resolve it)
5. **What a good outcome looks like** — what would you do with a good recommendation? What decision does it enable?
6. **Constraints** — time, resources, political realities, or out-of-scope items the team should know about
7. **What you're most uncertain about** — optional, but often the most valuable field: where is your own thinking weakest?

**Guidance for the Client:**
- Fill this in before the session, not in the moment. The brief is most useful when it's been thought through.
- Aim for 1 page. More than that and the situation hasn't been distilled.
- The "core question" field is the hardest and most important. If you can write one crisp sentence, the session will be significantly better.
- "What you've already tried" prevents the team from recommending what you've already ruled out.

## Role Cards
**URL:** `/library/session-kit/role-cards/`

Five cards. Everyone in the session holds one. Roles rotate across sessions — playing Client is at least as developmental as playing Consultant.

### Client
**Your job:** Present the problem clearly, stay available to answer questions during team work, receive the recommendation candidly, and participate fully in the debrief.
**Key behaviors:** Give the team a real problem — something you're actually wrestling with. Don't coach them during their work phase. Receive the recommendation as if it matters (because it should).
**Watch-out:** Holding back context that would change the analysis. If the team heads in the wrong direction, a clarifying question is fair game — but don't steer them toward your preferred answer.

### Lead Consultant
**Your job:** Own the team's process and the quality of the final recommendation. You're the one who stands up to present.
**Key behaviors:** Structure the team's work in the first 5 minutes. Assign branches. Keep one eye on the clock. Make sure the recommendation has a clear "so what" before you present it.
**Watch-out:** Doing all the thinking yourself instead of running the room. Your job is to integrate the team's work, not replace it.

### Analyst
**Your job:** Own the analytical heavy lifting. Choose and apply the right frameworks. Produce the evidence that tests the team's hypothesis.
**Key behaviors:** Reach for the most useful framework quickly. Develop findings that move the recommendation forward, not just interesting observations. Know when to stop analyzing and start synthesizing.
**Watch-out:** Analysis paralysis. The team needs conclusions, not data. At minute 50, you should be synthesizing, not still gathering.

### Skeptic
**Your job:** Make the team's thinking better by challenging it. Push back on assumptions, surface what's being ignored, and test whether the recommendation actually holds.
**Key behaviors:** Ask "what are we assuming here?" and "what would have to be true for this to be wrong?" Particularly useful in the first 15 minutes (is this the right question?) and the last 15 minutes (does this recommendation actually follow?).
**Watch-out:** Being contrary for its own sake. The Skeptic's goal is a stronger recommendation, not a stalled one. Challenge with purpose.

### Scribe
**Your job:** Capture the team's work in real time — on a whiteboard, on paper, or on screen — and keep the group honest about time.
**Key behaviors:** Build the issue tree and stakeholder map on the board as the team talks. Call out when you're 45 minutes in. Summarize findings as they emerge so the team doesn't have to reconstruct them.
**Watch-out:** Passive transcription. The best Scribes are active participants who happen to be holding the pen. If you see the team missing something, say so.

## Starter Cases
**URL:** `/library/session-kit/starter-cases/`

Five ready-to-run practice cases for sessions where no member has a live problem to bring. Each is intentionally varied in industry and problem type. The Client for any of these cases should spend 5 minutes reading the brief carefully before presenting — it helps to inhabit the role.

---

### Case 1: The Community Hospital Crossroads
**Industry:** Healthcare
**Problem type:** Capital allocation / strategy
**Frameworks to reach for:** Porter's Five Forces, Ansoff Matrix, Stakeholder Mapping, Balanced Scorecard

**Situation:** Mercy Regional is a 450-bed community hospital and the dominant provider in a mid-size metro area. The system has been profitable for a decade, but a large academic medical center 35 miles away recently announced plans to open a satellite oncology clinic in Mercy's market. Mercy's board is split on how to respond. One faction wants to invest $85M in building a new oncology center to compete head-on. Another faction argues for a formal partnership with the academic center rather than a costly new facility.

**The core question:** Should Mercy build the oncology center, and if so, should it go independent or partner with the academic center?

**Additional context:** Mercy's CFO notes that the $85M would be debt-financed at a time when operating margins have compressed from 7% to 4% over three years. The CEO believes oncology is where medicine is heading and that ceding this specialty would be strategically costly long-term. The board meeting is in six weeks.

---

### Case 2: The SaaS Slowdown
**Industry:** B2B Technology
**Problem type:** Revenue growth / churn diagnosis
**Frameworks to reach for:** Profitability Tree, Root-Cause Tools, SWOT, Marketing Mix / Jobs-to-be-Done

**Situation:** Vantage HR is a 400-person SaaS company serving mid-market HR and people operations teams. Eighteen months ago the company was growing at 40% year-over-year; today growth has fallen to 12%. Monthly gross revenue churn has risen from 1.8% to 2.4%. Leadership is split: the CEO believes it's a market saturation problem, the CRO thinks it's a sales execution problem, and the CPO argues the product has fallen behind.

**The core question:** What is actually driving the slowdown, and where should the company focus its efforts to return to growth?

**Additional context:** Net Revenue Retention remains strong (108%) among the top 20% of customers. New logo acquisition has declined more sharply than expansion revenue. A recent customer survey shows high satisfaction with core features but complaints about integrations and implementation time.

---

### Case 3: The Brand Under Siege
**Industry:** Consumer Goods
**Problem type:** Competitive strategy / brand positioning
**Frameworks to reach for:** Porter's Five Forces, Ansoff Matrix, Value Chain Analysis, Marketing Mix / Jobs-to-be-Done, Blue Ocean Strategy

**Situation:** Fieldstone is a regional snack and specialty food brand with strong loyalty in the Pacific Northwest. Eighteen months ago, a national DTC competitor launched a near-identical positioning — "wholesome, ingredient-forward snacks" — with a direct-to-consumer model that eliminates the retailer markup and allows 25% lower shelf prices. The competitor has grown 60% since launch. Fieldstone's retail sales are flat; margins have compressed as retailers demand increased trade spending to keep Fieldstone on shelf.

**The core question:** How should Fieldstone respond to the DTC threat without abandoning the retail relationships that built the brand?

**Additional context:** Fieldstone has loyal customers but limited digital presence. Its strongest asset is its brand story and community ties. It has not launched a DTC channel. Retail accounts represent 94% of revenue.

---

### Case 4: The Advisory Firm's Next Move
**Industry:** Professional Services
**Problem type:** Market expansion / growth strategy
**Frameworks to reach for:** Ansoff Matrix, Three Horizons, OGSM, Stakeholder Mapping, Business Model Canvas

**Situation:** Aldridge Partners is a 120-person management consulting firm based in Denver with a strong regional reputation in financial services and healthcare. The firm has been profitable for 12 consecutive years. Two growth paths are actively debated by the partners: (A) open offices in two adjacent markets (Phoenix and Salt Lake City), following the traditional geographic expansion model; (B) invest in building a virtual delivery capability and national digital presence, serving clients across the country without physical expansion.

**The core question:** Which expansion path should the firm pursue, and on what basis should the partners make this decision?

**Additional context:** The Denver market is becoming more competitive as national firms expand their regional footprints. Several senior partners have personal ties to clients in Phoenix. A junior partner made a compelling case for Option B at the last partners' retreat but was not taken seriously.

---

### Case 5: The Margin Squeeze
**Industry:** Specialty Manufacturing
**Problem type:** Profitability diagnosis
**Frameworks to reach for:** Profitability Tree, Root-Cause Tools (5 Whys / Fishbone), Value Chain Analysis, PESTEL

**Situation:** Apex Packaging is a specialty packaging manufacturer serving food and pharmaceutical companies. Over three years, EBITDA margin has declined from 18% to 11%, despite 8% annual revenue growth. The CFO has run preliminary analysis suggesting the problem may be on the cost side, but the CEO believes pricing pressure from customers is the primary driver. The board wants a clear diagnosis before next quarter's earnings call.

**The core question:** What is causing the margin decline — and what should management do about it?

**Additional context:** Raw material costs (resins and films) have increased 22% over the period. Headcount has grown faster than revenue. Two major customers (representing 35% of revenue) renegotiated contracts at lower prices 18 months ago. The company has not raised prices to smaller customers in two years.

## Timekeeper / Agenda Card
**URL:** `/library/session-kit/timekeeper-agenda/`

The standard 90-minute structure. Post this on the wall or keep it at the Scribe's station. The Scribe calls time at each beat.

**90-Minute Session:**
- **0:00 — Client brief (10 min):** Client presents using the Brief Template. Team listens without interrupting. Questions held until brief is complete.
- **0:10 — Clarifying questions (5 min):** Team asks factual questions only — not analysis, not recommendations.
- **0:15 — Problem reframe (5 min):** Team checks: is this the right question? Proposes a reframe if needed and confirms with Client.
- **0:20 — Structure the work (5 min):** Lead Consultant assigns branches of the issue tree. Hypothesis stated. Scribe captures the tree on the board.
- **0:25 — Parallel team work (45 min):** Each member works their branch. Analyst builds the evidence. Skeptic probes assumptions. Scribe captures findings as they emerge.
  - *0:45 — Midpoint check-in (5 min within the 45):* Team reconvenes briefly. Which branches are confirmed / eliminated? Is the hypothesis holding? Reorient if needed.
- **1:10 — Synthesis (10 min):** Team builds the recommendation together. Agree on the "so what." Lead Consultant prepares to present.
- **1:20 — Recommendation presentation (10 min):** Lead Consultant delivers to the Client. Concise, structured, direct. One recommendation with supporting logic — not a menu of options.
- **1:30 — Debrief (15 min):**
  - *0–5 min:* Client responds: was this the right question? Was the recommendation useful?
  - *5–10 min:* Team self-reflects: what worked? What would we do differently?
  - *10–15 min:* "Would I hire these people?" round — everyone answers, including the Client.

**Total: 1 hour 45 min.** Budget 15 min of flex for overruns and transitions; plan for a 2-hour block.

## Debrief & "Would I Hire You?" Scorecard
**URL:** `/library/session-kit/debrief-scorecard/`

A light rubric for the 15-minute debrief. Complete one per session — for the team as a whole, not individuals (the goal is collective feedback, not performance reviews).

**Five dimensions, rated 1–3:**
1 = Needs significant work | 2 = Solid, adequate in a real engagement | 3 = Strong, would stand out

**Problem Structure** — Did the team frame the right question? Was the analysis organized around a clear structure (issue tree, hypothesis, or equivalent)?

**Quality of Insight** — Did the team reach non-obvious conclusions, or did they largely restate the situation? Would this recommendation change how the client thinks or acts?

**Communication** — Was the recommendation crisp, structured, and delivered with appropriate confidence? Did it lead with the answer?

**Client Orientation** — Did the team listen to and engage the client, or did they present without connecting to what the client actually asked?

**Teaming** — Did the team work together effectively, or did it feel fragmented, dominated by one person, or missing a Skeptic voice?

☐ Conditionally — yes, with specific development on: _______________
☐ Not yet — because: _______________

## Facilitator's Guide
**URL:** `/library/session-kit/facilitators-guide/`

How to run a great Consulting Club session. This guide is for whoever is hosting — typically a member who isn't playing one of the five roles, or a rotating hosting duty.

**Before the session:**
- Confirm the Client has completed the Brief Template in advance, or block 15 minutes before the session starts for them to fill it in on paper.
- Set up a whiteboard or large paper at the front. The session needs a visible working space.
- Print or display the Agenda Card and Role Cards.
- Brief any new members on the format before the session starts — not during.

**Opening (first 5 minutes):**
- Frame the purpose: this is a low-stakes practice room. Nothing said here leaves the room. The debrief is candid by design.
- Introduce the roles, especially if there are new members. Remind everyone: playing Client is a real role, not a bye.
- Name the "Would I hire?" round explicitly at the start. Don't surprise people with it at the end — they need to know it's coming to engage honestly.

**During the work phase:**
- Stay out of the team's process. If they're going in a direction you'd push back on, let the Skeptic do their job.
- Watch the time. The most common failure mode is running out of time for the recommendation. If the team is still in analysis at minute 60, say so.
- If the team is completely stuck or headed irreparably in the wrong direction, you can pause and ask: "Does the Client want to give the team a hint?" — but use this sparingly. Some struggle is developmental.

**The recommendation presentation:**
- Hold the Client to genuine engagement. They should listen as if this is a real pitch, not perform politeness.
- Enforce the time. The team has 10 minutes. If they're still building up to the recommendation at minute 8, intervene: "What's the recommendation?"

**The debrief:**
- The debrief is the highest-value 15 minutes of the session. Protect it.
- Start with the Client's response — not the team's self-assessment. The Client's perspective anchors the conversation.
- Push for specificity. "That was pretty good" is not useful feedback. "The hypothesis was sharp but the final recommendation tried to cover too many options at once" is.
- Create space for the Skeptic and Scribe to reflect — these roles are easy to overlook in feedback.

**The "Would I hire?" round:**
- Go around the room. Everyone answers, including the Client and any observers.
- The question is: "Would I put this team in front of my actual client?" — not "were they nice" or "did they work hard."
- Model candor yourself. If the facilitator hedges, the room will hedge.
- After the round, invite one specific developmental observation from anyone who said "not yet" or "conditionally."

**Common failure modes:**

*The team skips the reframe.* They accept the Client's question as given and miss that it's the wrong one. Intervention: at minute 10, ask the team out loud: "Is this the right question?"

*The analysis never lands.* The team generates interesting findings but can't synthesize them into a recommendation. The Lead Consultant needs to force the issue at minute 60: "Given what we know, what's our recommendation — even if it's provisional?"

*The debrief stays polite.* Everyone says "that was great" and no one learns anything. Intervention: ask a specific, edged question: "What would have made this recommendation harder to argue with?"

*The Skeptic goes silent.* Either they ran out of things to push on, or they weren't willing to create friction. Either is worth naming in the debrief.

*The Client holds back.* They gave the team a sanitized version of the problem or withheld relevant context. This is worth naming: "If this had been your real situation, is there anything you would have told them that you didn't?"

**Closing well:**
- End by asking: "What is each person taking away?" One sentence per person.
- Note the next session's Client, date, and who's playing which role.
- The session is over when people feel they got better at something — not just when the clock ran out.
