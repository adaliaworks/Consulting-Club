Scaling Up

Four decisions every growing company must get right.

Overview

Verne Harnish is the founder of the Entrepreneurs' Organization (EO) and CEO of Gazelles, Inc., a global executive education firm. He first published Mastering the Rockefeller Habits in 2002, drawing on the operating discipline of John D. Rockefeller, who ran a vast enterprise through rigorous communication rhythms, data habits, and accountability. The updated and expanded version, Scaling Up: How a Few Companies Make It… and Why the Rest Don't (2014, Gazelles Inc.), is the definitive statement of the framework.

The framework's premise: most companies plateau not because of a flawed product or a bad market, but because leadership fails to get four decisions right simultaneously:

  • People — do you have the right people in the right seats, living the right values?
  • Strategy — does your strategy produce a differentiated brand promise that generates sustainable margins?
  • Execution — can the organization set and hit priorities quarter over quarter, and does everyone know whether they had a good day?
  • Cash — do you understand your cash conversion cycle and the levers that drive it?

Most executive teams are strong in one or two of these and weak in the others. Scaling Up argues that the constraint is almost always at the weakest link.

The Four Decisions in Depth

People

The People decision begins with Core Values — the three to five non-negotiable behaviors that define who belongs in the organization. These are not aspirational; they describe what the best people in the company already do. Adjacent to values is Core Purpose: the reason the organization exists beyond making money. Both are used in hiring, performance review, and the daily narrative of leadership.

The structural tool is the Function Accountability Chart (FACe), which lists every function the business must perform and names the single person accountable for it. In small companies, one person fills multiple functions; the chart makes the gaps visible. As the company grows, each function eventually needs a dedicated owner.

Strategy

The Strategy decision has seven components — Harnish calls them the 7 Strata of Strategy:

  1. Core Customer — a single well-defined customer type (not a segment, a person)
  2. Brand Promise — what you reliably deliver that competitors don't
  3. Brand Promise Guarantee — how you back it up when you fall short
  4. One-PHRASE Strategy — a short, memorable statement of your key differentiator
  5. Profit per X — the economic denominator that best explains how you make money (profit per seat, per customer, per pound, etc.) — borrowed from Jim Collins's Good to Great
  6. X-Factor — the underlying competitive advantage that is 10x-100x better than alternatives; the thing that would make competitors not want to compete with you in your chosen niche
  7. BHAG (Big Hairy Audacious Goal) — the bold 10-25 year goal, also from Collins; gives the strategy a long horizon beyond the quarterly and annual plan

Execution

The Execution decision is the most operationally specific. It has three components:

Priorities: Each quarter, the company names one Critical Number — the single metric that, if moved, will do the most to resolve the company's biggest current constraint. Around that, the team agrees on three to five Rocks: the most important milestones to hit by quarter end. Individuals set their own Rocks that roll up to the company Rocks.

Data: Every person and every team has a set of leading-indicator KPIs — not lagging measures like revenue, but the activities and inputs that predict it. The goal is that everyone can answer, in quantitative terms, whether they had a good day or week.

Meeting Rhythms: Harnish insists that communication problems are almost never about the wrong content — they're about the wrong cadence. The Scaling Up meeting rhythm:

  • Daily huddle (5–15 minutes, standing): what's up, where are you stuck?
  • Weekly team meeting (1 hour): KPI review, one educational topic, one issue resolved
  • Monthly leadership meeting (half day): deeper issue resolution, cross-functional alignment
  • Quarterly offsite (1–2 days): set next quarter's Critical Number and Rocks, address one constraint
  • Annual planning (2–3 days): set the year's theme, update the One-Page Strategic Plan

Cash

The Cash decision is about understanding your Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) — how many days elapse between spending money (on inventory, labor, or other inputs) and receiving payment. The shorter the CCC, the less working capital the business needs and the faster it can fund its own growth. Harnish's Power of One exercise asks: what happens to cash if you change each key lever (price, volume, COGS, receivable days, etc.) by just one unit? It makes the financial levers tangible for non-finance leaders.

The One-Page Strategic Plan (OPSP)

The signature deliverable is the One-Page Strategic Plan, a single page that aligns the full organization from values to this week's priorities. Reading left to right, it moves from the timeless (purpose, values, BHAG) through the long-range (3–5 year targets, 7 Strata) to the near-term (1-year priorities) and finally to the current quarter (Rocks, Critical Number). Every leader fills out the same template; the pages are compared to find misalignment before it becomes dysfunction.

Running It in a Session

Scaling Up works best as a diagnostic: take a real or fictional company and run it through the 4 Decisions to find the weakest link. Start by having the group vote on where the company is most constrained — People, Strategy, Execution, or Cash. You will almost always get disagreement, which is the point: the debate surfaces the real diagnosis.

For a 90-minute session, pick one decision to go deep on. If you choose Execution, work through the Critical Number together: given the company's biggest current challenge, what is the one metric the leadership team should be obsessing over this quarter? Then name three Rocks that would move it. This exercise is deceptively difficult — groups quickly learn that the hard part is choosing what not to prioritize.

If you have more time, try drafting a partial One-Page Strategic Plan for the company. Working from left to right (purpose → BHAG → 1-year priorities → current quarter) forces the team to test whether the long-range narrative and the near-term actions are actually connected — often they are not.

Common Pitfalls

  • Too many priorities — if the team names ten Rocks, none of them is really a Rock; the discipline is committing to three to five and protecting them from the quarter's noise
  • Treating the meeting rhythm as overhead — the daily huddle and weekly rhythm feel like wasted time until the alternative (email threads and miscommunication) is compared directly; the cadence is non-negotiable in Harnish's view
  • Aspirational core values — values that describe who you want to be, not who you already are, become poster slogans; the test is whether you'd fire someone for violating them
  • Critical Number chosen by consensus — if the metric is chosen because everyone can live with it, it probably isn't the real constraint; the Critical Number should make at least one executive uncomfortable
  • Skipping Cash — most leadership teams love People and Strategy conversations and avoid the Cash section; the CCC and Power of One exercises deserve equal time
  • Confusing the OPSP with a document — the One-Page Strategic Plan's value is in the alignment process of building it, not in producing a finished artifact; a team that copies last quarter's plan has missed the point
